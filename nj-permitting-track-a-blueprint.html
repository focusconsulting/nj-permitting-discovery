How a construction permit moves through DCA today

This is our approximation of how a construction permit moves through DCA today, one stage at a time, left to right, built from publicly available material. Each row is a different view: what the applicant does, what they see, what staff do behind the scenes, and the systems in play. Every cell is backed by a source, shown by the number in brackets and listed at the bottom. Some of it comes from the RFP and its Q&A; the rest we found in public records. Where we don't yet know, the bottom row carries the open question. The dark headers are the stages the RFP names; the lighter two at the end, build and close-out, come from the building code.

Which blueprint:
"Complex journey" shows the 9-stage blueprint below: a licensed professional, on work that's DCA-reserved or needs full plan review. "Simple journey" shows a shorter, separate blueprint for a homeowner on a minor residential alteration, roughly 89% of volume [18], since that path likely skips case-level DCA review. "RFP's 7 named stages" shows the Complex blueprint with Stages 8 and 9 hidden, build and close-out, which come from the building code rather than the RFP's own list.
Track B, upstream: where a project needs them, State prior approvals must be met before the construction permit can issue (5:23-1.4, 2.15(a)5, 2.16); plan review can proceed in parallel. The code names a DOT major access permit directly as one of these required-first approvals (highway curb cuts, 1.4). It doesn't name a DEP freshwater wetlands permit the same way, but we read it as covered by the same rule, since the code defines the category broadly enough to include it; that's our reading, not something DCA has confirmed. DEP decides within 90 calendar days after it rules the application complete (7:7A-19.7(b)); before that, its Letter of Interpretation (which tells the applicant whether wetlands are even present) runs 30 days if DEP needs no additional information, 45 days from when DEP gets whatever information closes the gap, and up to 45 more if DEP does a site inspection (7:7A-19.2, 19.7(a)). DOT confirms the application is complete within 15 days, then reviews it in 30 days for a minor permit or 90 for a major one (16:47-9.6). These feed the permit-issuance stage below (Stage 5).

Staff work
When the applicant explains the project at the counter, staff determine whether a Zoning Review or other prior approval is needed. [21] The application is taken in by hand; neither DCA system does intake. [11]
For DCA-reviewed plans, staff check the file is complete before review; the application still comes in by hand. [9][11] A 2016 manual shows the same step generically (completeness and prior-approval check before plan review is assigned); not confirmed as DCA's current process. [16]
Subcode officials review; plan review proceeds even if a prior approval is still pending. [1]
Re-review of the corrected plans in the same system. [9]
The construction official signs only after every subcode official approves; the permit issues only after DCA releases the plans, and DCA ensures the town gets a copy. Silence past 20 business days counts as a denial the applicant can appeal. [2][4]

Open questions
  • Where does a first-time applicant start a permit: by phone, at a counter, or with a form to download?
  • Which prior approval drives the schedule most?
  • Summit's own process starts with an in-person conversation before the applicant knows which form to fill out. [21] Is that counter-triage step common across towns, or specific to how Summit runs its office?
  • Summit checks a property for existing open permits before accepting a new application, since an open permit can delay approval. [21] Is that check universal, and how often does it block or delay someone?
  • How much of what applicants submit arrives on paper versus electronically?
  • What happens to the paper next, and how long does that take?
  • Do staff point applicants to one channel?
  • The code has required electronic applications since 2024-11 [17]; where paper still persists, what's enforcing or blocking compliance?
  • DCA's Q&A says staff rely on "the older system," and PermitsNJ, paired with ePlans, is what the proposal draft names as that older system. [19] If PermitsNJ is gone, what are staff using now instead of EnerGov?
  • How are deficiencies communicated to the applicant, and how clearly?
  • Which subcode reviewer holds up a decision most often?
  • Summit's own FAQ names two delay causes directly: missing or illegible paperwork at submittal, and applicants or their hired professionals not returning calls promptly. [23] Do these hold up as the main causes elsewhere, or is Summit's experience different from DCA's own reviews?
  • For any professionals who tried digital submission and went back to paper, what happened?
  • What do staff dislike about the newer system?
  • Which checks can subcode staff run as simple arithmetic, and which need context or judgment?
  • DCA has 20 business days to decide; silence past that lets the applicant treat it as a denial and appeal. [4] Does DCA track that 20-day window internally? Has any applicant used the right to treat silence as a denial, or does it go unused?
  • Does fee collection still happen at separate points (issuance, certificate, variation), each logged by hand, or has it consolidated? Does a Tax Assessor notification still fire when a permit fee is collected? [16]
  • Can an applicant check status without calling?
  • When someone does call, what can the staff member see, and in which system are they looking, EnerGov, ePlans, or something else?
  • For a self-enforcing town like Cherry Hill, does DCA-reserved work bypass the town's own intake, or start there and get forwarded? If forwarded, does the town's staff keep any role afterward, and does the applicant ever find out which one happened?
  • SDL and EnerGov are separate vendor systems with no confirmed integration between them [15]; when a town's staff need to get a file to DCA, do they have a system to do it with, or are they re-keying or emailing it by hand?
  • Mullica relinquished to DCA's Southern Regional Office in February 2026. [12] Is intake there paper only, and is that DCA's default for any relinquished town? For a Mullica applicant today, who do they call with a question, the town or DCA, and does either side know the answer?
  • Where a handoff exists, which staff member ends up re-entering something someone else already typed, and does the applicant ever see that delay reflected back to them?
  • DCA plays two roles: reviewing certain plan classes directly, and running full permitting for towns that have relinquished enforcement to it. Does the same DCA employee have to switch between two different ways of working depending on which town's file just landed on their desk?
  • Is inspection scheduling digital, or still phone and paper?
  • Where do inspections stall?
  • Which of the 2016 manual's steps (logs, stickers, violation notices) still run this way today, whether the work is handled by DCA directly or by a town's own building department? [16] Have any of these steps changed since the 2024 electronic-application mandate? [17]
  • How is the certificate requested?
  • How long does the applicant wait from start to certificate?
  • Does the 2016 manual's paper trail (fee log, permit file, central file) still run this way today, whether the work is handled by DCA directly or by a town, and has it changed since the 2024 electronic-application mandate? [16][17]
A homeowner doing minor residential work at a self-enforcing town likely skips case-level DCA review: no plan review, no subcode sign-off, no DCA release. That much comes from a 2016 procedures manual's Minor Work branch [16], not from any current digital system. DCA still receives this permit in aggregate, though: every municipality, including self-enforcing ones, must report monthly permit activity to DCA. [20] We haven't confirmed whether minor work can ever fall into a DCA-reserved class, whether this 2016 process still matches how any specific town handles minor work today, or whether "no case-level review" is the same thing as "no DCA involvement" once that reporting requirement is counted.

Open questions
  • Does any current system, SDL, FastTrackGov, ePlans, flag Minor Work separately at intake, or is this distinction paper-only and possibly gone?
  • For a self-enforcing town, does DCA ever see any part of this path, or does it stay entirely local?
  • Is direct issuance, without a plan review cycle, still real anywhere, or has it merged into the standard permit flow now that applications are electronic?
  • Is there any check-in during construction, or is the completion notice the only touchpoint?
  • Is 30 days still the real window? Does an inspection-scheduling system, if one exists, treat Minor Work differently from milestone inspections?
  • Does "Certificate of Approval" still exist as a document distinct from "Certificate of Occupancy," or have these merged?
  • What happens on a failed inspection specifically for Minor Work?
To confirm at kickoff: every specific town in this blueprint, Cherry Hill, Mount Laurel, and Summit, is self-enforcing, not DCA-served; the counter-triage step, the delay causes, the status tracking, all of it comes from towns where DCA's role is limited to occasional reserved-class review and aggregate reporting. The actual DCA-served population, the 50-plus towns DCA runs permitting for directly, has exactly one example so far, Mullica, and all that's confirmed there is that it relinquished in February 2026 and DCA's regional office reviews paper only. What a DCA-served town's front door, staff work, and applicant experience look like is still almost entirely unknown, and that gap should shape who we interview first. Beyond that: the outputs of DCA's event-storming sessions on the DEP wetlands and DOT major access permits, so Track B can build on it; whether DCA's two roles, reviewing certain plan classes directly and running full permitting for towns that have relinquished enforcement to it, share the same systems and staff; whether the current systems were built to auto-check the code, since that shapes what a computer can decide; whether a shorter Minor Work path and a separate Variation application [16] still exist in current systems, given roughly 89% of volume is small residential alteration work under $5,000 [18]; whether homeowner-as-agent and professional-as-agent map onto that same simple/complex split, or vary independently; and how DCA staff, municipal staff, licensed professionals, and residents each experience this process differently.
