How Track B feeds into Track A

This is our approximation of how two State prior approvals feed into the Track A construction permit, built from publicly available material listed at the bottom. If a project touches a state highway or a wetland, the applicant needs a second approval from a different state agency before DCA can actually hand over the construction permit: DOT for the highway, DEP for the wetland. The construction code calls this a "prior approval," since it has to happen before DCA's own permit can issue. DCA can still review the building plans while a prior approval is pending, it just can't issue the final permit until the approval comes through. The code names the DOT highway approval directly as a prior approval. It doesn't name the DEP wetlands approval the same way, but we read it as covered by the same rule, since the code defines the category broadly enough to include it. That's our reading, not something DCA has confirmed. 5:23-1.4, 2.15(a)5, 2.16

A delay in either prior approval delays when the construction permit can issue. The rule doesn't say whether DEP or DOT usually causes the bigger delay for a given project. We'd have to ask people who've actually gone through it to find out.

Worth flagging for DCA

A DEP wetlands applicant can never assume silence means yes: past the deadline, the rule says they still can't treat it as approved. [24] Track A's construction permit works the other way: silence past 20 business days lets the applicant treat it as a denial and appeal. If a project needs both, the applicant is on two different clocks with two different rules for what silence means.

Applicant does
Requests a Letter of Interpretation to check whether wetlands are present. Picks the right permit tier, gives public notice, then files with the fee. [24]
Figures out which permit category applies, based on how much traffic the project will generate. Requests a pre-application meeting where required, and prepares a traffic study. [25]
Agency actor
DEP's Land Use Regulation Program issues the LOI, confirms the tier, and reviews the application. [24]
NJDOT's Bureau of Major Access Permits holds the pre-application meeting. If the applicant pays to have DOT look at the site early, before the full application is filed, DOT responds within 30 days. [25]
Decision rule
DEP has 90 days once the application is complete to decide. That can stretch 30 more days, but only if both sides agree in writing. If DEP goes silent past the deadline, the applicant can't assume it's approved. [24]
Once the application is complete, DOT reviews it: 30 days for a smaller project, 90 for a larger one. If DOT finds something missing, the applicant gets 90 days to respond, then DOT takes another 90 days to look again. [25]
Systems
NJDEP Online (njdeponline.com) files wetlands applications electronically. [26]
MAP E-Permitting (njdotepermits.net) tracks the application end to end. [25]
Open questions
  • How many applicants pass DEP's completeness check on the first try, and what makes DEP reject the rest?
  • The property owner and the engineer they hired both wait on the LOI before the wetlands application can even start. How long does that wait run?
  • How many applicants clear DOT's first technical review, and how many get sent back? Each cycle costs the applicant 90 days to respond, then DOT another 90 days to look again.
  • The applicant pays a traffic engineer to prepare the impact study before DOT's clock even starts. How much of the total wait is that study? DOT's own process wouldn't touch that part of the clock.

USEPA transmittal and clock suspension for major discharges at 19.5, 19.7(e). Read directly from the operative text at law.cornell.edu, 2026-07. DEP's Land Use Regulation Program is confirmed, across DEP's own permit forms and independent sources, as the administering body for wetlands permits and Letters of Interpretation.
  • [25] N.J.A.C. 16:47: category by trips at 2.1; review-path selection at 9.5(a), confirming one-step is 9.6 and two-step is 9.7; pre-application meeting and traffic impact study at 9.4(b)-(e), including the $1,000 study-area pre-review fee credited to the application fee; one-step completeness and review at 9.6(a)-(d) (15-day completeness, 30-day minor / 90-day major review, 90+90 deficiency loop).
